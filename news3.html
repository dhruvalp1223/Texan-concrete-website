    Concrete Quality Can Vary Widely Based on Its Specific Mixture

    Concrete is the backbone of construction, and its strength and quality depend upon the composition of its various components. Chemical admixtures, cement, and aggregates are carefully blended to create the ideal mixture for each construction project. Once mixed with water, this blend becomes the durable substance that has made possible the towering skyscrapers and sturdy bridges that dominate modern architecture.

    With so many variables in the composition of concrete, standards are essential for quality control. Even small variations in the composition of concrete can have significant impacts on its durability and strength. Without adequate checks and balances in place, you could inadvertently use an inferior concrete supply for your Houston commercial projects, resulting in costly repairs, safety hazards, and damage to your company’s reputation.

    ASTM Concrete Standards Set Criteria for Quality Concrete Supply

    American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) cement and concrete standards set the expectations and requirements for our industry. These standards are essential in the testing and evaluation of concrete, cement, and aggregates. They are in place to ensure that these materials meet the necessary requirements for a variety of applications.

    From construction projects to infrastructure developments, the ability to test the strength and safety of concrete mixtures is crucial. Thanks to the ASTM standards, laboratories all over the world have a universal benchmark to measure against, providing accurate and reliable results.

    These standards help to identify the various properties of concrete including:

    • Strength: This attribute of concrete defines its ability to resist applied loads without breaking or cracking. The strength of concrete is largely determined by the ratio of water to cement in the mix, with less water typically resulting in stronger concrete.
    • Elasticity: This refers to the ability of concrete to deform and then return to its original shape once a load is removed. A higher modulus of elasticity indicates a stiffer concrete mix. The elastic properties of concrete are primarily determined by the stiffness of the aggregate and the cement matrix.
    • Hardness: Hardness is the measure of concrete’s resistance to scratching, abrasion, or indentation. A harder concrete surface is more resistant to wear and tear, making it suitable for flooring and pavement applications.
    • Workability: This attribute refers to how easy it is to mix, place, compact, and finish the concrete. Workability is crucial as it affects the concrete’s strength, durability, and appearance. Factors affecting workability include water content, aggregate size and shape, and the use of admixtures.
